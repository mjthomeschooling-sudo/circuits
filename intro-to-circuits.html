THE SPARK: UNCOVERING THE BASICS OF ELECTRICITY

What This Stage Was About

This first stage introduced my student to the foundational ideas behind electrical circuits before we moved into simulations and hands‑on tools. We explored circuits through a mix of BBC Bitesize articles, videos, mini‑games, and quizzes, along with the Bill Nye Electricity episode. These resources gave him multiple ways to understand the same ideas — reading, watching, interacting, and testing his knowledge — which helped build confidence before moving into more complex tools.

Alongside these digital resources, he took notes in his digital notebook, using the Kindle Scribe to handwrite diagrams and vocabulary. This let him sketch naturally while still staying in a fully digital workflow.

This stage mattered because it gave him the confidence and vocabulary he needed before we moved into simulations and hands‑on circuits.

My Reflection

As I worked through this first lesson, I gained a better sense of how to introduce circuit concepts in a way that feels accessible rather than overwhelming. The guided questions, note‑taking, and short BBC quizzes helped reinforce ideas, and I could see the student growing more confident as the repetition settled in. There were moments of confusion around terms like potential difference and resistor, but using diagrams and everyday comparisons helped clear those up and showed me how important it is to anchor new vocabulary in something familiar.

The lesson also highlighted a few practical adjustments I need to make moving forward. The videos I had tested earlier suddenly stopped working, and the pacing took twice as long as I expected, which reminded me to build in more flexibility and backup options. Even with those challenges, the student stayed engaged, asked questions, and enjoyed drawing the diagrams. By the end, he had a solid sense of what would and would not work in a circuit, and he was ready to move into the PhET simulation with enough understanding to make predictions and choose between pictures or diagrams confidently.

Student Reflection

“Before this lesson, I only knew that electricity could shock me and that it powered things like lights and electronics. After watching the videos, I learned a lot more about what electricity actually is, like how electrons move, what amps and voltage mean, and the difference between series and parallel circuits. Series circuits made the most sense to me because they’re just one simple loop. Potential difference was confusing at first, but once I saw a diagram, it finally clicked.

I also started noticing how this stuff shows up in real life. In my room, the wall switch turns on both the light and the fan, but the pull chain only changes the fan, and now I understand why. I feel mostly confident about electricity after this lesson, and I’m still curious about what other things use electricity besides the ones we talked about.”
