DREAM IT, DRAW IT, WIRE IT: PROTOTYPING THE MASTER PLAN

What This Stage Was About

After completing his introduction to the Micro:bit, my student moved into the design phase of the project. In this stage, he brainstormed possible features for the final house and then built multiple prototypes of each idea. For every feature, he created a Micro:bit‑controlled version and, whenever possible, one or more standalone circuit versions. This helped him decide which approach would be the best fit for the final house.

This stage mattered because it pushed him to make evidence‑based decisions and think like an engineer.

Tools We Used

Micro:bit PICRAT: Creative–Transformation

A programmable microcontroller used to prototype smart features and automation.

MakeCode PICRAT: Active–Transformation

Used to write and simulate block-based code for the Micro:bit prototypes.

Standalone Circuits PICRAT: Passive–Replacement

Physical circuits built without a controller to provide a simple, direct-wired alternative for house features.

My Reflection

This lesson reminded me how important it is to slow down during early prototyping so the student has time to make sense of the components before moving into design decisions. The T‑edge connector and pin layout showed me where clearer explanations and visuals are needed, and the doorbell troubleshooting reinforced the value of letting him rebuild rather than stepping in too quickly. Even with those challenges, the hands‑on testing helped me see what will realistically work in the foam house and how to support him as we move into installation.

Student Reflection

“In this lesson I tried out a bunch of different electronic ideas for my foam house, like a doorbell, servo doors, a motion light, a fan, window blinds, light switches, and an intruder alarm. I learned that some projects worked better when I used the micro:bit and the breadboard, and other projects were actually easier to build without them. I was surprised that some of the setups looked complicated at first but ended up being really simple once I built them.

I had a few challenges, especially with the doorbell. When I was testing it, the micro:bit kept acting like it was disconnecting and reconnecting even though it was still plugged in, and the only thing that fixed it was taking the whole circuit apart and rebuilding it. The code was easy to understand, and after practicing more I finally understood how the T‑edge connector and its pins worked, but they were confusing at first. Testing everything helped me see how much space the parts will take up in my house. I’m still trying to figure out how to hide the servo in the door or window frame, and I might change my plan for the blinds since I have so many windows. I feel mostly confident about installing the electronics in my foam house next.”

ISTE Standards Addressed

1.1 Empowered Learner

The student used Micro:bit code, breadboard wiring, and standalone circuits to explore electronics at their own pace—testing inputs, outputs, sensors, and actuators while taking ownership of how each feature brought their foam house to life.

1.4 Innovative Designer

The student designed and built functional house features—doorbells, lights, motion sensors, alarms, and servo‑powered doors—iterating on both code and wiring to solve problems, refine behavior, and meet design constraints.

1.5 Computational Thinker

The student broke down each project into logical steps, created sequences and conditionals in MakeCode, and used systematic troubleshooting to diagnose issues in both code and physical circuits, applying clear reasoning to achieve working solutions.

1.6 Creative Communicator

The student communicated their design choices through circuit diagrams, photos, wiring explanations, and code walkthroughs, clearly describing how each electronic feature functioned within the foam house.
